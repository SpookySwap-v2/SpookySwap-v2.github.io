SpookySwap Overview: Mechanics, Routing & Liquidity

SpookySwap is Fantom’s battle-tested AMM and liquidity hub. It offers two pool archetypes — volatile (constant-product) for standard pairs and stable (curve-like) for correlated assets. The router selects paths across these pools to balance output versus gas, minimizing price impact for a given trade size.

Pool Types & When They Shine

  • Volatile pools: Best for non-correlated assets (e.g., FTM/ALT). Classic x*y=k pricing; deeper liquidity reduces slippage.
  • Stable pools: For highly correlated tokens (e.g., USDC/USDT, wstETH/stETH). Tighter curve near parity → improved execution for stable pairs.
  • Fee tiers: Select pools with sensible fee tiers for your pair; lower tier helps majors, higher tier may compensate LPs on riskier pairs.

Router Priorities (What It Optimizes)

  • Expected output net of gas: More hops can improve price but cost more gas; the router weighs net outcome.
  • Path quality: Stable/volatile mix, avoiding dust pools, and keeping price impact small for your size.
  • Execution reliability: Tight slippage while avoiding unnecessary reverts under normal volatility.
Tip: On Fantom, gas is inexpensive but not free. For smaller trades, prefer simpler routes (1–2 hops). For larger trades, check if splitting across stable + volatile pools improves the effective price.

Security Posture & User-Side Protections

SpookySwap is non-custodial, but approvals and routing still require diligence. Treat every transaction like production: reduce trust assumptions, validate contracts, and keep keys safe. Below are pragmatic guardrails that materially reduce risk without compromising UX.

Wallet & Approval Hygiene

  • Use a hardware wallet for serious balances; verify on-device recipient, amount, and contract.
  • Approve the minimum required (or a tight ceiling) instead of unlimited allowances.
  • Revoke stale approvals on FTMScan or a reputable allowance tool; schedule a monthly review.
  • Bookmark the official app and docs; avoid links from random DMs or search ads.

MEV-Aware Flow

  • Use tight but realistic slippage; widen a touch during volatile windows to reduce failed txs.
  • Consider private/builder RPC if supported by your setup to mitigate sandwich risk.
  • For large orders, split into chunks; smaller footprints are harder to exploit.
  • Prefer stable pools for stable pairs to avoid unnecessary curve exposure.

Threat Model (What Actually Breaks Users)

  • Impostor tokens / wrong contracts: Always match token addresses from the project’s official channels.
  • Approval abuse: Unlimited allowances to unknown contracts create long-lived risk; minimize and rotate.
  • Route drift & price swings: If the market moves beyond tolerance, tx reverts; refresh quotes and retry.

Remember: the cheapest transaction is the one you don’t need to resubmit. Small test swaps de-risk execution.

Smart Routing Across Stable & Volatile Liquidity

The router can hop through canonical assets (e.g., wFTM) and combine stable with volatile legs. This is especially helpful when direct pools are shallow. Evaluate both the quoted output and the number of hops to understand net cost.

Limit Orders & DCA (When Available)

  • Limit orders: Target a specific price and let the system execute when conditions are met. Expect partial fills or expiry in choppy markets.
  • DCA: Time-diversify entries/exits. Ensure your wallet stays funded with FTM for each leg.

LP & Farming (Liquidity Provider Perspective)

  • Fee income: Earn swap fees proportional to your pool share; stable pools often see tighter spreads and high volume on pegged pairs.
  • Impermanent loss: Volatile pools can out-earn fees or underperform depending on path of prices; model scenarios before committing size.
  • Pool selection: Prefer pairs with durable demand, sensible fee tier, and sustained volume; avoid fragmented or vampire-attacked markets.

Developer Notes

  • Expect deterministic calldata and clear revert semantics on min-amount checks.
  • Use event logs for route, hops, realized output, and gas cost to drive analytics/BI.
  • Stable-pool math differs near parity; test edge cases for very small deltas.

Practical Runbook: Getting Great Fills on Fantom

Before You Trade

  • Keep FTM for gas (approvals + swap + potential retry).
  • Verify token addresses + decimals; beware “fee-on-transfer” tokens.
  • Start with a micro-swap to validate route behavior.

Slippage & Gas

  • Majors & stable pairs: 0.1–0.5% slippage; long-tail assets: wider tolerance.
  • Raise priority fee during volatility to reduce inclusion delay.
  • Prefer fewer hops for small tickets; explore stable routes for stables.

For Large Size

  • Split orders across time or pools to flatten impact.
  • Check both stable and volatile alternatives; compare net output.
  • Monitor pool health (depth, recent volume, fee tier alignment).

Troubleshooting Matrix

  • INSUFFICIENT_OUTPUT_AMOUNT → Price moved or slippage too tight. Refresh quotes; nudge tolerance slightly; consider chunking.
  • TRANSFER_FROM_FAILED → Missing approval or token restrictions. Re-approve minimal amount; confirm token supports standard transfers.
  • Pending forever → RPC congestion or too-low tip. Speed up with higher priority fee or switch RPC.
  • Weird output → Confirm you used the correct token contract and pool; inspect hops in the route preview.
Execution KPI: Track effective price = (received units / cost) accounting for gas + pool fees. Comparing routes by effective price (not just quoted output) improves decision quality.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is SpookySwap different from aggregators?

Aggregators search across many venues (sometimes cross-chain) and may split routes. SpookySwap focuses on deep, native Fantom liquidity with gas-efficient routing across its own stable/volatile pools. For Fantom pairs with strong local depth, SpookySwap often wins on realized execution + gas simplicity.

Which tokens and networks are supported?

SpookySwap is Fantom-native (FTM). Tokens supported depend on existing pools and listings. Always confirm contract addresses on FTMScan or the project’s official channels before swapping or LP-ing.

How should I set slippage?
  • Stable pairs: 0.1–0.3% most of the time (liquidity-dependent).
  • Liquid majors: 0.2–0.5% typical; volatile markets may require a nudge.
  • Long-tail pairs: set based on depth and recent volatility; start conservative and scale into the position.
How do I reduce MEV / sandwich risk?
  • Use tight but realistic slippage to leave less extractable value.
  • Prefer private/builder RPC (if your setup supports it) for meaningful trades.
  • Split large orders; adjust gas to reduce time in mempool.
Can I provide liquidity and what should I watch?

Yes. Choose pools with persistent flow and sensible fee tiers. Model impermanent loss versus fee income, particularly on volatile pairs. Stable pools are well-suited for correlated assets where IL is structurally lower.

My transaction failed — what now?
  • Re-quote; markets may have moved.
  • Increase priority fee or switch RPC if pending too long.
  • Verify allowances and contract addresses; re-approve the minimum if needed.
